How to Dye

The recipes throughout this website are yours to use. We invite you to follow them precisely or to treat them as a starting point — forage for plants in your own landscape, adapt to your local season, and make the process your own.

Please read the following before you begin.

Safety Notice

Natural dyeing is a rewarding practice, but it requires care. Many plants — including some edible ones — are toxic when heated or handled in quantity. Rhubarb leaves, for example, release oxalic acid when boiled. Always check any plant for toxicity before use.

  • Wear gloves throughout.
  • Work in a well-ventilated space — outdoors is ideal.
  • Never use kitchen equipment for dyeing. Keep all pots, spoons, and vessels dedicated to dye use only.
  • Do not inhale steam or vapour.
  • Keep children, pets, and food away from your dye space.

All activities are undertaken at your own risk. Please ensure you research materials thoroughly and follow safe working practices at all times. The project accepts no responsibility for injury, loss, or damage arising from the use of this information.

What You'll Need

  • Stainless steel pot (non-reactive)
  • Long-handled spoon (dye use only)
  • Thermometer
  • Heat source
  • Rubber gloves
  • Large tub for soaking

Aluminium and copper pots can also be used experimentally and often shift colour results in interesting ways.

Understanding Mordants

A mordant is a substance that bonds dye to fibre, creating colours that last. Without mordanting, most natural dyes will fade quickly.

Throughout this project we used aluminium potassium sulphate (alum) as our mordant — a naturally occurring mineral salt, safe in the small amounts used in hand dyeing, and found in everyday products from deodorant to food preservatives.

We follow Margaret Redpath's reduced-impact method, using 10% WOF (weight of fibre) — lower than many standard recipes and gentler on waterways.

Protein fibres (wool and silk) absorb mordants directly and require only an alum bath. Cellulose fibres (cotton and linen) require a two-step process: a tannin pre-treatment (we use oak gall tannin, locally sourced where possible) followed by the alum bath.

Both aluminium potassium sulphate and oak gall tannin can be purchased online.

Dyeing Methods

Across this project, we worked with a range of natural dye techniques depending on plant material, season, and desired result. Below are four core approaches you can use at home.

1. Hot Dye Extraction (Fresh Plant Material)

This is the most direct method, used for many fresh seasonal plants.

Fresh plant material is harvested and used immediately. Fibre is first thoroughly wetted for around one hour before dyeing to ensure even absorption.

Plant material is added to a dye bath and gently heated (typically around 80–90°C) for 60–90 minutes, allowing colour to slowly release into the water. Fibre is then added and kept at temperature until the desired depth of colour is reached.

Once complete, fibres are rinsed thoroughly and hung to dry.

2. Overdyeing & Colour Shifting with Modifiers

Modifiers are used to shift or deepen colour after dyeing, revealing hidden tones within plant dyes.

After dyeing and rinsing, fibres can be immersed in modifier baths such as:

  • Ferrous iron (iron water) — often darkens and mutes colours, shifting them towards greys, olives, and deeper tones.
  • Citric acid or vinegar — can brighten or slightly shift acidity-sensitive dyes.
  • Soda ash (alkaline bath) — can push colours towards warmer, more golden or altered hues depending on the dye source.

Fibres are typically immersed briefly (from a few minutes up to 10–15 minutes depending on the desired effect), then rinsed and dried.

Modifying is best approached slowly — colour changes can be immediate and dramatic.

3. Fermented Indigo Vat (Fresh Plant Material)

This method uses fresh indigo-bearing plants to create a natural fermentation vat.

Plant material is macerated in warm water and left in a sealed container for several days. As fermentation develops, the vat becomes active and the pigment is released and transformed into a soluble dye.

Before use, the vat is gently stirred or agitated to activate it. Fibres are fully wetted beforehand and then dipped in short, repeated immersions (typically three 5-minute dips), allowing oxygen to develop the colour between each immersion.

Fibres are then rinsed and hung to dry. Colour continues to deepen as it oxidises.

4. Fructose Indigo Vat (Powdered Indigo)

This method uses pre-prepared powdered indigo combined with a reduction system to make the dye soluble.

A vat is created using indigo powder and a reducing agent (commonly a sugar-based system such as fructose, with an alkaline component). The mixture is gently warmed and left until the liquid turns a clear yellow-green, often with a visible surface bloom.

Once ready, fibres are fully wetted and dipped briefly (around 2 minutes per immersion), then removed to oxidise in the air before repeating if a deeper colour is desired.

Fibres are then rinsed and hung to dry.
